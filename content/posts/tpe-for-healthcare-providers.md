Therapeutic plasma exchange is one of the hottest anti-aging procedures around and for good reason. Over the last twenty years a wave of research has shown that the removal and dilution of old blood is able to significantly slow the aging process and may actually decrease biological age.

Many anti-aging and regenerative medicine clinics are considering bringing therapeutic plasma exchange into their practices. While it may appear to be a simple process, there are many factors that need to be considered.

This article is designed to give the basics of bringing TPE aka PLEX into your practice, but is far from comprehensive.

## Clinical Landscape

The first thing to consider is whether your target market needs and can afford the procedure. While there are over 100 indications described by The American Society For Apheresis in its most recent guidelines, most of these scenarios are best served in the hospital setting either because of their acute presentation or established payor networks. Autoimmune diseases like Guillain-Barré, chronic inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy, and multiple sclerosis come to mind.

In the outpatient world, most clinics are looking at serving those interested in general health and longevity, post-viral syndromes like Long-COVID, mild cognitive decline, and Alzheimer's disease. There is a growing body of evidence for these indications, however, most hospital systems routinely turn these people away even when they offer to pay cash.

Insurance companies will typically not pay for these procedures and the patient will have to pay out of pocket at a cost of anywhere from $5,000 per procedure to $25,000 per procedure, depending on the clinic.

## Why Is It So Expensive?

Once you've determined if your patient mix will be able to benefit and afford the procedure, you will need to decide whether or not you can afford to offer it.

While I don't have exact figures I will provide a rough estimate of the costs. First, you'll need the machine. There are two FDA-approved machines available in the United States, the Fresenius Kabi Amicus and the Spectra Optia by Terumo. Each machine is over $100,000 brand new. They also require disposable kits that cost several hundred dollars a piece. You'll need tubing, IV sets, and needles/catheters. You'll also need albumin as the replacement fluid, which can run close to $1,000 per procedure depending on how much you wish to exchange. My rough estimate of per procedure costs, excluding nursing salaries, doctor salaries, and the cost of the machine is about $1,500 per procedure.

If you're an already established clinic, you're already paying the overhead costs like rent and you can lower overall cost further by obtaining training for your own nurses to run the procedure. If you're looking to launch a clinic solely to offer PLEX, you'll need to consider all the additional costs.

## Staffing Requirements

You'll need a physician or advanced practice provider to supervise the procedure as well as a nurse to run the procedure. It is best practice to have the nurse stay with the patient for the entirety of the procedure to monitor for side effects and track vitals every 15-20 minutes. Many clinics prefer to have two nurses available as some complications are best handled with a second pair of hands.

It is important to obtain sufficient training for all staff. While the procedure appears simple enough, it is a medical procedure that comes with a certain amount of risk and requires adequate medical knowledge and technical skills. Staff should be well informed about the proper indications for the procedure, all contraindications, timing of the procedure, selecting appropriate parameters, and adverse events. The supervisor should be familiar and comfortable with the machine, but the nurse is typically the technical expert.

There are several options for training clinical staff, including those offered by established practices or machine manufacturers that provide training on their specific machines. I've personally been very happy with the training and customer service provided by Fresenius Kabi.

## Safety Considerations

Patient safety protocols are crucial. You'll need comprehensive pre-procedure screening protocols, emergency response procedures, and detailed documentation systems. Understanding contraindications is essential -- conditions like severe cardiovascular disease, active bleeding, or inadequate vascular access can pose significant risks.

## Patient Selection and Protocols

For longevity and wellness applications, patient selection becomes more nuanced than traditional medical indications. You'll want to establish clear protocols for evaluating candidates, including comprehensive health assessments, laboratory evaluations, and realistic expectation setting.

Consider developing treatment protocols that specify exchange volumes, replacement fluids, and session frequency. Many clinics start with conservative approaches and adjust based on patient response and emerging research.

## Implementation Timeline

### Phase 1: Planning and Preparation (3-6 months)

- Market analysis and financial projections
- Regulatory compliance research for your state
- Equipment procurement and facility modifications
- Staff hiring and initial training arrangements
- Insurance and liability considerations

### Phase 2: Setup and Training (2-3 months)

- Machine installation and testing
- Comprehensive staff training completion
- Protocol development and safety procedures
- Initial patient identification and screening
- Marketing and referral network development

### Phase 3: Launch and Optimization (3-6 months)

- Pilot procedures with carefully selected patients
- Protocol refinement based on initial experience
- Staff competency validation
- Quality assurance implementation
- Volume building and operational efficiency

## The Reality Check

Here's the bottom line -- implementing TPE is not trivial. The upfront costs are substantial, the regulatory requirements are complex, and the learning curve is steep. Many practices underestimate the operational complexity and find themselves in over their heads.

The successful clinics I've observed typically have either extensive experience with similar procedures or access to expert guidance during implementation. The margin for error is small when you're dealing with expensive equipment, complex procedures, and patient safety.

## Getting Expert Help

Most practices benefit significantly from experienced consultation during the planning and early implementation phases. Whether it's navigating equipment selection, developing protocols, training staff, or troubleshooting operational challenges, having access to someone who's already solved these problems can save both time and money.

The TPE field is evolving rapidly, with new research emerging regularly and protocols being refined based on clinical experience. Staying current with best practices while avoiding costly mistakes typically requires more expertise than most practices can develop internally.

For providers seriously considering TPE implementation, connecting with experienced practitioners can provide valuable insights into feasibility, realistic timelines, and successful launch strategies. The complexity of this procedure makes expert guidance not just helpful, but often essential for success.
