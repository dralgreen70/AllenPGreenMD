## The Amyloid-Negative Finding: A Game-Changer

Perhaps the most important finding from AMBAR came not from the original 2020 publication, but from a follow-up analysis published as a Letter to the Editor in 2022.

### The Discovery

When the AMBAR investigators analyzed CSF samples to determine amyloid status, they made a surprising discovery:

**Of the 298 patients with available biomarker data:**

- **~70% were amyloid-positive** (CSF A-beta-42/A-beta-40 ratio <0.05)
- **~30% were amyloid-negative**

This happened because AMBAR enrolled patients based on clinical diagnosis of Alzheimer's disease (using NINCDS-ADRDA criteria), not based on biomarker confirmation. This was a phenotypic diagnosis—if the patient had progressive memory loss and cognitive decline consistent with AD, they were eligible.

About 30% of AMBAR patients (roughly 90 people) were amyloid-negative—yet they benefited just as much as amyloid-positive patients. This means the treatment works through mechanisms beyond just removing amyloid plaques.

When they analyzed treatment response by amyloid status, **both amyloid-positive and amyloid-negative patients showed similar clinical benefit.**

Specifically, amyloid-negative patients showed statistically significant improvements compared to controls in:

- ADAS-Cog (cognitive function)
- CDR-sb (global function)
- ADCS-CGIC (clinical impression of change)

### Why This Is a Major Finding

The AMBAR trial enrolled patients based on clinical symptoms of Alzheimer's disease, without requiring positive confirmation of amyloid pathology via imaging or biomarkers. As a result, 30% of participants, roughly 90 people, were found to be amyloid-negative. Yet exploratory analyses showed that these amyloid-negative patients benefited from plasma exchange treatment just as much as the amyloid-positive ones, with significant improvements in cognitive and functional measures (e.g., ADAS-Cog, CDR-Sb, and ADCS-CGIC) at 14 months compared to placebo.

This has profound implications:

**1. It's not just about amyloid**

If plasma exchange worked solely by removing amyloid from the brain, it shouldn't work in amyloid-negative patients. The fact that it does means other mechanisms are at play.

**2. It addresses a critical unmet need**

Current FDA-approved treatments (lecanemab, donanemab) require amyloid-positive biomarker status. If you have an Alzheimer's phenotype but are amyloid-negative, you have no treatment options.

The CTAD Task Force (Clinical Trials on Alzheimer's Disease) specifically highlighted this gap: patients with AD-like symptoms but negative amyloid biomarkers represent an "unmet need" that current therapies don't address.

Plasma exchange appears to work for these patients.

**3. It suggests broader applicability**

If the treatment works through multiple mechanisms—reducing inflammation, oxidative stress, vascular dysfunction, removing various toxic proteins—it might benefit other neurodegenerative conditions beyond just amyloid-driven AD.

**4. It changes how we think about treatment targets**

The pharmaceutical industry has spent 20+ years and tens of billions of dollars targeting amyloid. The implicit assumption has been: remove amyloid plaques, cure Alzheimer's.

The AMBAR results, particularly the amyloid-negative finding, suggest we should be targeting the multiple downstream consequences of aging and neurodegeneration rather than focusing solely on upstream amyloid accumulation.

*If plasma exchange works through multiple mechanisms—reducing inflammation, oxidative stress, vascular dysfunction—it might benefit other neurodegenerative conditions beyond just amyloid-driven Alzheimer's.*

## Why Haven't You Heard About This?

If plasma exchange showed effect sizes of 52-71% with minimal safety concerns, and these findings have been independently replicated, why isn't this the standard of care for Alzheimer's disease?

That's a reasonable question with several answers.

### 1. Industry Dynamics

The monoclonal antibodies are backed by major pharmaceutical companies (Biogen, Eisai, Eli Lilly) with massive marketing budgets and extensive relationships with prescribing physicians. These companies spent billions on drug development and clinical trials, have dedicated sales forces calling on every neurologist, run direct-to-consumer advertising campaigns, fund continuing medical education programs, and support patient advocacy organizations.

AMBAR was sponsored by Grifols, a company that manufactures therapeutic albumin. While Grifols is a substantial company in the transfusion medicine and plasma protein therapeutics industry, they don't have a large presence in neurology, don't have a neurology sales force, have a more limited marketing budget, and are promoting an off-label use of existing products rather than a patented new drug.

The economic incentive structure heavily favors the monoclonal antibodies despite potentially inferior efficacy and safety.

### 2. Regulatory Pathway

The monoclonal antibodies went through the traditional FDA drug approval pathway. They are now FDA-approved indications, which means physicians can prescribe with confidence, insurance companies are required to cover them (in many cases), there's a clear standard of care, and legal liability is reduced.

Plasma exchange with albumin replacement remains an **off-label use** of existing approved products. Both plasma exchange and albumin are FDA-approved, but not specifically for Alzheimer's disease. This creates practical barriers: some physicians are hesitant to use treatments off-label, insurance coverage is less certain, reimbursement may be denied, and implementation requires more institutional support.

### 3. Practical and Logistical Barriers

Prescribing lecanemab or donanemab is straightforward: write a prescription, the patient goes to an infusion center every 2-4 weeks, the infusion takes 30-60 minutes, and it is relatively simple to implement.

Plasma exchange is more complex. It requires specialized apheresis equipment, trained apheresis nurses and technicians, takes 2-3 hours per session, needs appropriate venous access, requires coordination between neurology and apheresis services, and many medical centers don't have apheresis capabilities on-site.

It's simply easier to prescribe a drug than to implement a procedural intervention.

### 4. Medical Conservatism and Paradigm Shift

Neurologists are trained to think in terms of pharmaceutical interventions. Plasma exchange represents a different paradigm—a procedural, extracorporeal therapy more commonly associated with hematology or nephrology.

There's natural conservatism in medicine around adopting new treatment modalities, especially when they fall outside the typical scope of practice. A neurologist prescribing an antibody infusion feels familiar. Referring a patient for repeated plasma exchange sessions feels foreign.

### 5. Study Size and Replication Lag

While AMBAR was well-designed and adequately powered, it was smaller than the pivotal monoclonal antibody trials:

- AMBAR: 322 patients
- Lecanemab: 1,795 patients
- Donanemab: 1,182 patients

In medicine, there's often a desire to see large-scale replication before widely adopting a new treatment. The Argentina real-world study provides important validation, but it was even smaller (32 patients) and used historical controls rather than concurrent randomization.

A large, multicenter, randomized controlled trial specifically comparing plasma exchange to standard of care (or to monoclonal antibodies) would probably be needed to shift widespread practice.

### 6. Complexity of Mechanism

Monoclonal antibodies have a simple, compelling story: "Remove the toxic plaques that cause Alzheimer's."

Plasma exchange has a more complex story: "Remove damaged proteins, reduce inflammation, restore antioxidant capacity, improve vascular function, and clear various toxic factors through multiple mechanisms that we don't fully understand yet."

The first story is easier to communicate, even if the second might be more accurate and effective.

### 7. Economic Incentives for Healthcare Systems

Here's an uncomfortable truth: the monoclonal antibodies are very expensive—around $26,000-28,000 per year for the drug alone, plus costs of PET scans, MRI monitoring, and infusion services. This generates significant revenue for healthcare systems.

Plasma exchange with albumin replacement is also expensive, but the reimbursement structure is different and potentially less favorable to hospitals and health systems in some settings.

## Limitations and Uncertainties

In the interest of balanced reporting, let's acknowledge the limitations of the AMBAR study and the uncertainties that remain.

While AMBAR showed impressive results, it's important to acknowledge limitations: industry sponsorship, single trial (until Argentina validation), and smaller sample size than mAb trials. Science requires honest evaluation of both strengths and weaknesses.

### Study Limitations

**1. Industry sponsorship:** The study was funded by Grifols, which manufactures therapeutic albumin. While the trial was independently conducted with oversight from academic investigators and a Data Safety Monitoring Committee, the potential for bias exists.

**2. Single trial:** Until the Argentina study, AMBAR was a single trial. While the results were internally consistent and showed effects across multiple outcomes, medicine generally requires replication before establishing new treatments.

**3. Sample size:** At 322 patients, AMBAR was adequately powered for its primary endpoints but smaller than the pivotal trials for competing therapies.

**4. Treatment arm ambiguity:** None of the three treatment arms (low-albumin, low-albumin+IVIG, high-albumin+IVIG) showed clear superiority when analyzed separately. The significant effects were seen when all three were pooled. This makes it unclear which specific protocol is optimal.

**5. Dropout rates:** While similar to other AD trials, the dropout rates of 34-35% in the IVIG-containing arms and 20-22% in the non-IVIG arms are substantial. This likely reflects the procedural burden and potential side effects from IVIG.

**6. Biomarker inconsistency:** The CSF biomarker results don't provide a clear mechanistic explanation for the clinical effects, particularly in mild AD patients.

**7. Blinding challenges:** While the study used sham plasma exchange to maintain blinding, procedural studies are inherently challenging to blind perfectly. However, the high completion rate in the placebo group (80%) suggests blinding was reasonably effective.

### Remaining Uncertainties

**1. Optimal treatment protocol:**

- How many intensive phase sessions are needed?
- What's the ideal maintenance schedule?
- What plasma exchange volumes are necessary?
- Is IVIG beneficial, neutral, or harmful?

**2. Treatment duration:**

- The benefits were still emerging at 14 months—how long should treatment continue?
- What happens if treatment is stopped? Do benefits persist or fade?

**3. Patient selection:**

- Who benefits most? Mild or moderate AD? (Evidence suggests moderate)
- Does APOE4 status matter?
- Should treatment start earlier or later in disease course?
- Would it work in MCI (mild cognitive impairment)?

**4. Mechanism of action:**

- Is it primarily amyloid clearance, anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, vascular, or some combination?
- Why do amyloid-negative patients benefit?
- What are the actual target molecules being removed?

**5. Cost-effectiveness:**

- The treatment is resource-intensive—is it cost-effective compared to alternatives?
- What's the real-world economic impact?

**6. Long-term outcomes:**

- Does it delay nursing home placement?
- Does it extend survival?
- What happens beyond 14 months?

**7. Combination therapies:**

- Could plasma exchange be combined with other treatments?
- Would it synergize with monoclonal antibodies or other approaches?

## Conclusion

The AMBAR study demonstrated that plasma exchange with albumin replacement can slow cognitive and functional decline in Alzheimer's disease patients, with effect sizes of 52-71% across multiple outcome measures. These findings have been independently validated in a real-world cohort from Argentina.

### Clinical Results

- Clinical benefit: Plasma exchange showed statistically significant and clinically meaningful effects on activities of daily living (52% less decline, p=0.03) and strong trends on cognitive measures (66% less decline, p=0.06)
- Global function: The effects were even stronger on comprehensive functional measures, with 71% less decline on CDR-Sum of Boxes (p=0.002) and essential stabilization on clinical global impression (p<0.0001)
- Moderate AD benefit: The strongest and most consistent effects were seen in patients with moderate Alzheimer's disease (MMSE 18-21), showing 61% less decline across cognitive and functional measures
- Mild AD pattern: In mild AD patients, global functional measures showed actual improvement from baseline rather than just slowed decline
- Safety profile: Approximately 90% of procedures were uneventful, with no ARIA risk—a significant advantage over monoclonal antibody therapies
- Independent validation: The 2025 Argentina real-world study replicated the key findings, showing 45% less cognitive decline and 74-88% preservation of memory
- Comparative efficacy: AMBAR's effect sizes (52-71%) exceed those of FDA-approved monoclonal antibodies (26-40%) with superior safety

### Looking Ahead

The AMBAR findings—particularly the amyloid-negative response and multi-factorial mechanism—suggest plasma exchange may be addressing fundamental aging processes rather than just Alzheimer's-specific pathology. This has profound implications for longevity and healthspan optimization beyond neurodegenerative disease.

**The mechanism remains incompletely understood.** The biomarker data suggest plasma exchange may work through multiple pathways—reducing inflammation, oxidative stress, and vascular dysfunction while potentially preventing new amyloid deposition rather than just removing existing plaques. The fact that it benefits amyloid-negative patients supports a broader mechanism of action.

**The practical reality is complex.** Despite promising results, plasma exchange hasn't become standard of care due to regulatory pathways, logistical challenges, limited awareness, and the practical difficulties of implementing a procedural intervention in a field accustomed to pharmaceutical treatments.

*The AMBAR study may have stumbled onto something bigger than anyone realized.*
